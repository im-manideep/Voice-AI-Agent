# LLM-as-Judge Pitfalls

## The pattern and why it's used

LLM-as-judge uses a strong model to grade outputs — scoring answers on a
rubric, comparing two candidates, or checking claims against sources. It
scales where human review cannot: it is cheap per judgment, fast, and
consistent enough to catch regressions in CI. But a judge is itself a model
with failure modes, and treating its scores as ground truth without validation
is the root mistake behind most bad eval pipelines.

## Known biases

Position bias: in pairwise A/B comparisons judges favor one position (often
the first answer); the fix is to run both orderings and count a win only if it
survives the swap. Verbosity bias: longer, more elaborate answers score higher
than equally-correct concise ones. Self-preference (narcissism) bias: a model
grading outputs tends to prefer text generated by itself or its own family.
Style-over-substance: confident, well-formatted prose scores higher than
hesitant but correct prose; judges can be fooled by authoritative tone.
Score-band collapse: on a 1–10 scale judges cluster scores in a narrow band
(most things get a 7), so absolute scores are less informative than relative
comparisons. Prompt sensitivity: reworded rubrics shift scores, so a rubric
change invalidates comparisons with older runs.

## Design mitigations

Prefer pairwise comparison over absolute scoring where possible — models are
better at "which is better" than "how good is this". Swap positions and
average. Use a rubric with concrete, binary criteria ("cites at least one
retrieved passage: yes/no") instead of vague quality scales. Ask for the
reasoning before the verdict — chain-of-thought before the score measurably
improves judge agreement with humans. Constrain the output format (strict
JSON with an enum verdict) and parse defensively: judges emit malformed JSON,
extra prose, or scores outside the scale, so validate and retry once on a
parse failure rather than crashing or silently coercing garbage. Keep the
judge model fixed and versioned for a given eval set, and use a judge from a
different family than the system under test when self-preference matters.

## Calibration against humans

Before trusting a judge, measure its agreement with human labels on a sample —
even 50 hand-labeled examples reveal systematic bias. Report agreement
(Cohen's kappa or simple percent agreement), and re-calibrate whenever the
judge model or rubric changes. A judge that agrees with humans 90% of the time
on a validation slice can be trusted to rank experiments; a judge never
validated cannot be trusted for anything beyond smoke testing. For high-stakes
decisions, use the judge as a filter and route disagreements or low-confidence
cases to human review.

## What breaks in practice

Typical production failures: an eval suite whose scores are all 7/10 and never
move (band collapse hiding real regressions); a "quality improvement" that is
actually verbosity bias rewarding longer outputs; CI flakiness from
non-deterministic judges (fix temperature at 0 and pin the model version); and
grading answers without giving the judge the retrieved context, which makes
faithfulness judgments impossible — the judge must see the same evidence the
system saw.
